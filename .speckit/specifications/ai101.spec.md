## [title] Introduction to AI

**Subtitle**: A practical, safe, and useful overview for business + IT audiences

**Notes**: Open with a quick framing: this is not a hype talk. We'll cover what AI is, where it helps across functions, and the guardrails needed in a regulated environment.

Set expectations: we'll keep it accessible for business users while giving IT enough structure to align on governance and implementation realities.

---

## [content] Why AI matters now

- AI is becoming a general-purpose capability across every function
- Biggest wins come from improving real workflows—not isolated demos
- Business + IT must co-own outcomes: value, risk, and operating model

**Notes**: Use 1–2 quick examples everyone relates to: summarizing long documents, drafting communications, finding answers across policies.

Emphasize: AI isn't 'an IT project' or 'a business toy'—it's both.

---

## [content] What is AI (plain English)

- AI = systems that learn patterns from data to make predictions or generate outputs
- Different from traditional software (explicit rules) → AI learns from examples
- AI can assist with language, images, speech, forecasting, and recommendations

**Notes**: Avoid jargon. If you need a one-liner: AI helps computers do tasks that usually require human judgment—by learning from data.

Transition: there are different 'flavors' of AI.

---

## [content] A quick evolution (30 seconds)

- Rules & expert systems → machine learning → deep learning → foundation models
- We're now in the era of 'natural language interfaces' for many tasks
- The shift: from automation → reasoning assistance → action via tools

**Notes**: Keep it short. The point is not history—it's that interfaces changed. People can interact in plain language, which expands who can benefit.

---

## [content] Three categories you'll hear (and why they matter)

- Predictive AI: forecasts / scores (risk, fraud, utilization, staffing)
- Perception AI: reads/listens/sees (OCR, speech-to-text, vision)
- Generative + Agentic AI: drafts, summarizes, reasons, and can take actions

**Notes**: Anchor: most enterprise solutions combine these. Example: document intake (perception) + classification (predictive) + summarization (generative).

---

## [content] Generative AI (GenAI) in one slide

- GenAI creates text, summaries, code, and explanations from prompts
- Strength: speed + drafting + synthesis across lots of information
- Limitation: it can be wrong—needs grounding, review, and guardrails

**Notes**: Explain the 'copilot' mental model: GenAI is excellent at first drafts and synthesis. The human is still accountable.

Set up next: why outputs can be wrong and what to do about it.

---

## [content] From chatbots to agents

- Chatbots: answer questions
- Copilots: assist within workflows
- Agents: plan multi-step work, call tools/APIs, follow policies, and report back

**Notes**: For the mixed audience: business users should think 'workflow helpers'; IT should think 'software that can call other systems'.

Key idea: agents require stronger controls than chat.

---

## [two-column] AI Myths vs Reality

**Left**:
- Myth: AI is a single product you 'install'
- Myth: GenAI outputs are always correct
- Myth: AI will replace most jobs quickly
- Myth: More data/models automatically means better outcomes
- Myth: Governance slows innovation

**Right**:
- Reality: AI is a capability applied to workflows, data, and people
- Reality: GenAI can be wrong—use grounding, citations, and review
- Reality: Near-term wins come from augmentation + new roles/skills
- Reality: Quality, context, and evaluation drive reliable results
- Reality: Governance enables safe scaling in regulated environments

**Notes**: Quickly reset expectations so the rest of the talk lands.

Read 2–3 myths that you've actually heard in the org. For each, state the reality and the implication: what we do differently (governance, grounding, evaluation, humans in the loop).

Bridge: now that expectations are set, let's talk about where AI helps across the enterprise.

---

## [content] Enterprise AI vs consumer AI

- Enterprise AI must respect privacy, security, and access controls
- Regulated data (e.g., PHI) requires strong boundaries and auditing
- Success depends on adoption, process change, and operating model

**Notes**: Distinguish: consumer AI is 'best effort'; enterprise AI must be dependable.

Emphasize: permissioning and data boundaries are non-negotiable in healthcare.

---

## [content] Where AI helps across departments (common patterns)

- Summarize: meetings, documents, cases, tickets
- Search + synthesize: policies, procedures, knowledge bases
- Draft: emails, reports, plans, FAQs
- Classify/route: documents, requests, exceptions
- Automate: repetitive steps with human approvals

**Notes**: This is the 'everyone wins' slide. Keep it concrete and universal.

Tell the audience: if you're not sure where AI fits, start by listing tasks that are text-heavy, repetitive, or decision-support oriented.

---

## [content] Examples: HR

- Policy Q&A assistant (grounded in approved HR content)
- Onboarding copilot: checklists, role-based FAQs, training paths
- Drafting: job descriptions, interview guides, internal comms

**Notes**: Stress: HR use cases must be careful about bias and fairness.

Recommend: start with 'content + drafting' and add automation only after governance is in place.

---

## [content] Examples: Finance & Accounting

- Invoice / expense exception triage and narrative explanations
- Variance analysis: summarize drivers and suggest follow-ups
- Close process assistant: checklists, status roll-ups, drafting

**Notes**: Frame value: faster cycles, fewer errors, better consistency.

Add control: outputs should be traceable to source data; AI shouldn't 'make up' numbers.

---

## [content] Examples: Insurance Ops + CenterWell

- Claims/appeals packet summarization + next-step suggestions
- Utilization management (UM): intake → summarize → route with guardrails
- Knowledge surfacing: reduce duplication and contradictions in content

**Notes**: Keep it non-medical: we're improving operations and documentation workflows, not replacing clinician judgment.

Emphasize: human review remains essential.

---

## [content] Examples: IT & Engineering

- Developer copilots: faster coding, testing, documentation
- Modernization: migration assistance, refactoring, code review
- IT ops: incident summarization, runbook drafting, search across logs/docs

**Notes**: For IT: mention evaluation and guardrails as 'quality gates' for AI outputs.

Highlight: wins require standards (prompting patterns, templates, policies), not one-off experiments.

---

## [content] Risks (and why they're manageable)

- Privacy/security: sensitive data exposure
- Incorrect outputs: hallucinations or outdated guidance
- Bias & fairness: uneven impacts across groups
- Over-automation: removing needed human judgment
- Operational risk: model drift and changing behavior

**Notes**: Be calm and practical: risks are real but solvable with the right controls.

Transition: responsible AI principles + concrete guardrails.

---

## [content] Responsible AI (simple version)

- Fairness
- Reliability & Safety
- Privacy & Security
- Inclusiveness
- Transparency
- Accountability

**Notes**: Use this as a mental checklist for every use case.

Say explicitly: people stay accountable. AI assists; humans own decisions and outcomes.

---

## [content] Practical guardrails that make AI safe

- Data boundaries: what can/can't be used (especially PHI)
- Access controls: role-based permissions and least privilege
- Grounding: cite sources; prefer verified knowledge
- Human-in-the-loop: approvals where it matters
- Monitoring & evaluation: test quality, detect drift, audit

**Notes**: Translate to both audiences: for business, these are 'safety rules'; for IT, these are 'controls and operational requirements'.

If asked 'where to start': start with grounding and human review.

---

## [content] How to start (a 30-day playbook)

- Pick 1–2 workflows with clear pain (time, cost, quality)
- Define success metrics (time saved, accuracy, compliance, satisfaction)
- Start with assist → then partial automation with approvals
- Establish a lightweight governance loop (security, compliance, owners)
- Pilot, measure, iterate, then scale

**Notes**: Make it actionable: ask each team to nominate one workflow and one owner.

Close the loop: adoption comes from measured wins and safe scaling.

---

## [content] Close: 3 takeaways

- AI is an enterprise capability—value comes from real workflows
- Business + IT must co-own outcomes and guardrails
- Start small, measure impact, and scale responsibly

**Notes**: End with an invitation: identify candidate workflows and schedule a short follow-up working session to prioritize.

Optional: leave 5 minutes for Q&A and capture questions that need follow-up.
